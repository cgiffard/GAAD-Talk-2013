What we Built

If you ask me, the most accessible video service there is.

  • Keyboard accessible
  • ARIA-Live Support (really tailored)
  • Complete captions support in browser native formats
  • Mobile device support
  • Bandwidth responsiveness & multiple resolutions
  • Load balanced, fast and secure (node.js behind varnish)
  • Embeddable and distributable anywhere
  • Audio descriptions, alternate video tracks
  • Fully interactive transcript and chapters
  • Automatic transcoding
  • Responsive design
  • Live thumbnail preview when reading transcript
  • MediaFragments to link straight to content
  • Fast start streaming
  • And much more...

But most importantly:

It looks great.

(And works wonderfully too.)

Roadblocks

  • Cost of producing captions
  • Turnaround time
  • Resistance to new processes
  • Lack of understanding
  • Technical challenges

And none of these problems are totally solved.

We deal with this by...

  • Working with caption and transcription providers
  • Educating stakeholders
  • Investing time in making systems easier to use
  • Learning, building, and deploying new technology
  • Providing a great user experience!

Technology

When we started, the technology wasn't there.

We worked around that by building a lot of it ourselves.

But now, there's lots to choose from!

What about for me?

If you have no development expertise, YouTube is actually reasonably good. (Hardly perfect, though.)

If you do, there's a checklist of things you'll need to satisfy.

Checklist

  • Delivers video using HTML5. Not optional.
  • Flash fallback for IE8 (in ~1-2 years, won't be required and should be removed.)
  • Video available in both WebM and H.264
  • Video player is responsive and completely keyboard accessible.
  • Offer the video and caption files for download as a fallback
  • Provide a transcript and captions using an open standard.

As a general guide, no part of your stack, at all, ever, should use a proprietary technology.
